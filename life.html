Beyond the Research

Life Outside the Lab

I study the atmosphere for a living — but I'm equally interested in why we've collectively failed to act on what we already know. This page is about the other things that shape how I think.

Public Speaking & Debate

Arguing for a living, before research took over

Before I was arguing about climate models, I was arguing in committee rooms. I served as an Executive Board member at Model United Nations conferences — the side of MUN that requires you to understand every delegate's position, manage a room full of competing agendas, and distil hours of debate into something that moves forward.

It taught me something research doesn't always: that a well-framed argument matters as much as a correct one. You can be right and still lose the room. That lesson travels.

I was also President of IISER Bhopal's Debating Society — building a culture where people felt comfortable defending uncomfortable positions, which turns out to be useful training for science too.

Sport & Movement

The only thing I take as seriously as deadlines

Sport is where I switch the analytical part of my brain off. It works about 60% of the time.

I run regularly — partly for fitness, partly because it's the one hour of the day where nothing requires a citation. I also play Pickleball, Table Tennis, and Squash, all at a level best described as "competitive but humbling."

There's something I find genuinely useful about sport: it gives you immediate, unambiguous feedback. You win or you don't. The ball was in or it wasn't. Research almost never works that way, so I appreciate the clarity.

Ideas

Philosophy I actually think about

Not as a discipline — as a set of live questions that keep showing up in my work whether I invite them or not.

💡
Enlightenment & Public Reason
Kant argued that the failure to think for yourself is a kind of self-imposed immaturity. Climate inaction often has exactly this character — not ignorance of facts, but deference to authority, short-term interest, or habit. My paper on Kantian climate ethics tries to take this seriously rather than just reference it.
⚖️
Collective Action & Moral Responsibility
Who is responsible when harm is spread across billions of people and generations? Climate change is the hardest version of this question. I'm interested in how political philosophy and decision theory can give more honest answers than standard cost-benefit economics usually does.
🌍
Epistemic Justice in Science
Whose knowledge gets counted? My work on GCM credibility over the Global South is partly a scientific claim and partly a philosophical one: that the way we build and evaluate models encodes assumptions about which regions matter. That is not a neutral technical choice.
Science Communication

The gap between knowledge and policy is a design problem

Scientists are trained to generate knowledge. They are rarely trained to communicate it to the people who make decisions with it. I find that gap more interesting — and more consequential — than most technical problems I work on.

The things I've done in this space so far are modest, but they're pointing somewhere I want to go further.

📰
The India Forum — Policy Commentary
Co-authored a piece arguing that North India's air crisis cannot be solved by focusing only on stubble burning and fireworks — the sources are more complex and the policy conversation needs to catch up. Published October 2025.
🎙️
Chrysalis Podcast — Climate Governance
Appeared on a podcast to discuss climate governance and the political dimensions of geoengineering. Talking about science to a non-specialist audience forces you to find out what you actually understand versus what you can only repeat.
🌐
GeoMIP 2024 — Cornell University
Presented research on stratospheric aerosol injection to an international audience of climate scientists. Communicating uncertainty honestly — without undermining the seriousness of the findings — is a skill I'm still developing.
