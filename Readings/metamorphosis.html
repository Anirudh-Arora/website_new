Reflections on Usefulness, Isolation, and Quiet Replacement

A Reflection on Franz Kafka’s The Metamorphosis

Franz Kafka’s The Metamorphosis is often introduced as an absurd story — a man waking up as an insect. But very quickly, the absurdity fades, and what remains feels disturbingly familiar.

What struck me most was not Gregor Samsa’s transformation itself, but how little it actually changed anything fundamental. Even after waking up as a bug, Gregor’s first thoughts are not about fear, identity, or freedom, but about work — missing the train, disappointing his employer, and failing his family. His sense of self is so deeply tied to usefulness that even a complete physical transformation cannot interrupt it.

Initially, I felt an overwhelming sadness for Gregor. He is isolated, misunderstood, and physically incapable of communicating his intentions. But as the story progressed, my sympathy became more complicated. The family’s response — especially their gradual emotional withdrawal — felt cruel at first, but eventually it felt painfully rational. They are not villains. They are exhausted people adapting to a new constraint.

What disturbed me most was the moment when the family realizes that they can, in fact, survive without Gregor... This calculation is devastating precisely because it is reasonable. From that point on, Gregor is no longer central to the household’s functioning. His presence becomes logistical rather than emotional, a problem to be managed rather than a life to be cared for.

By the time Gregor dies, the family’s lack of mourning no longer feels shocking — it feels like the natural conclusion of a process that had already mentally removed him from their world. His death is not treated as a loss, but as a release. That, more than anything else, made the story feel strangely normal.

Reading The Metamorphosis also forced me to reflect on how modern life quietly isolates people through routine and obligation. There were moments where Gregor’s isolation felt uncomfortably relatable — times when being busy with work creates emotional distance from family, not through conflict, but through absence. Kafka doesn’t dramatize this isolation; he normalizes it, which makes it harder to dismiss.

One question that stayed with me after finishing the book was whether anything would have truly changed if Gregor had recovered. I don’t think it would have. He would have returned to the same routine, the same expectations, the same conditional value. The tragedy of the story is not the transformation itself, but the realization that no transformation was needed for Gregor to become expendable.

The Metamorphosis doesn’t offer solutions or moral clarity. Instead, it sharpens awareness — of how systems quietly replace individuals, how usefulness can overshadow dignity, and how adaptation can look indistinguishable from indifference. It is an unsettling read, not because it is strange, but because it feels honest.
