Literary Reflection

Reflections on Usefulness, Isolation, and Quiet Replacement

A reflection on Franz Kafka's The Metamorphosis

Anirudh Arora
Climate Researcher · IISER Bhopal / KAUST
Fiction Franz Kafka Philosophy

Franz Kafka's The Metamorphosis is often introduced as an absurd story — a man waking up as an insect. But very quickly, the absurdity fades, and what remains feels disturbingly familiar.

What struck me most was not Gregor Samsa's transformation itself, but how little it actually changed anything fundamental. Even after waking up as a bug, Gregor's first thoughts are not about fear, identity, or freedom, but about work — missing the train, disappointing his employer, and failing his family. His sense of self is so deeply tied to usefulness that even a complete physical transformation cannot interrupt it.

The Problem of Sympathy

Initially, I felt an overwhelming sadness for Gregor. He is isolated, misunderstood, and physically incapable of communicating his intentions. But as the story progressed, my sympathy became more complicated. The family's response — especially their gradual emotional withdrawal — felt cruel at first, but eventually it felt painfully rational. They are not villains. They are exhausted people adapting to a new constraint.

What disturbed me most was the moment when the family realizes that they can, in fact, survive without Gregor. Not just survive — but perhaps live better. This calculation is devastating precisely because it is reasonable. From that point on, Gregor is no longer central to the household's functioning.
— Personal reflection

The family's adaptation is a form of moral accounting that we rarely articulate but constantly perform. How much of our care for others is genuinely unconditional, and how much is contingent on what they contribute — financially, emotionally, practically?

On Usefulness and Identity

The deeper reading is about the fragility of identity built on utility. Gregor's identity before the transformation was almost entirely defined by his role as a provider. He had no real inner life that we see — only obligations. The transformation does not create his isolation; it merely makes it visible. He was, in a sense, always a bug to the system that consumed his labour.

There is something here that resonates with how young academics and researchers experience their own worth. In competitive, output-driven environments, the tendency to tie one's value to productivity — papers published, grants secured, projects delivered — is almost universal. Gregor's story is a warning about what that looks like taken to its logical conclusion.

How the Story Ends

Gregor dies, and the family experiences something close to relief. His death frees them. The final scene — the family taking a tram into the countryside, the parents noticing their daughter's youth and beauty — is quietly devastating. The world moves on. The grief is absent. Life optimises around the absence.

I don't think Kafka meant this as a cynical indictment of the family. I think he meant it as an honest portrayal of how systems — families, institutions, economies — adapt and continue. The tragedy is not that they are cruel. It is that they are ordinary.

Would anything have been different had Gregor stayed human? I don't think it would have. He would have returned to the same routine, the same expectations, the same conditional value. The tragedy of the story is not the transformation itself, but the realization that no transformation was needed for Gregor to become expendable.
— Personal reflection

What It Left Me With

The Metamorphosis doesn't offer solutions or moral clarity. Instead, it sharpens awareness — of how systems quietly replace individuals, how usefulness can overshadow dignity, and how adaptation can look indistinguishable from indifference.

It is an unsettling read, not because it is strange, but because it feels honest. And honesty, in Kafka's hands, is rarely comfortable.
